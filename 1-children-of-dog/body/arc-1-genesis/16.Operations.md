# Operations

> "No battle plan survives first contact with the enemy."  
> &mdash; Helmuth von Moltke

---

The first controlled experiment nearly killed Dr. Marcus Chen.

The young scientist had approached the captive hive with appropriate caution, his equipment checked and his protocols approved. Autumn's sensor arrays monitored everything—temperature, chemical composition, growth rates, the subtle electromagnetic signatures that marked antithesis biological processes. Dr. Kellerman observed from a safe distance, her xenobiology expertise making her both fascinated and appropriately wary.

What nobody had anticipated was the speed. Textbook descriptions of antithesis growth patterns were derived from well-fed hives in favorable conditions. This valley specimen, struggling in nutrient-poor soil against actively hostile eucalyptus, had developed an opportunistic desperation that showed when Chen moved within striking distance.

The dog weed erupted from concealment with velocity that made Autumn's warning redundant—by the time she'd processed threat parameters and alerted, the thing was already airborne. Chen stumbled backward with a shout, his careful scientific detachment replaced by instinctive panic as several kilograms of alien muscle and teeth oriented toward his throat.

The Flesh Melter detonated mid-leap, Forest's throw intercepting the creature with timing born of practice rather than calculation. It wasn't the small unit, it was the big one normally used to clear a house sized area, and the throw was absurdly precise, right into its open mouth, triggering a reflexive swallow. It detonated inside the unfortunate beast, the dog weed literally dissolving in mid-air and spattering across Chen's hazard suit in corrosive gel that smoked against the protective material.

Silence stretched across the clearing while Chen processed how close he'd come to becoming antithesis lunch. Then he vomited inside his helmet with the gut-deep reaction of someone whose body had registered danger before his mind caught up.

"New protocol," Forest announced to the assembled researchers while Chen was helped out of his contaminated suit. "Nobody approaches active specimens without armed escort. I don't care if you think it's contained, if sensors say it's dormant, or if your grandmother swears it's perfectly safe. Antithesis are evolved predators. They're always more dangerous than you think."

Dr. Kellerman nodded, her xenobiology background making her appreciate the lesson. "Adaptive behavior in response to environmental stress. We've been studying well-fed specimens in controlled conditions. These valley variants are operating under selective pressure for aggressive opportunism."

"Which means they're desperate, unpredictable, and considerably more dangerous than your textbooks suggest," Forest agreed. "Autumn, new rule: any human within twenty meters of active antithesis gets armed escort and continuous tactical monitoring. No exceptions."

> _Implementing now. Also designing containment protocols that assume maximum threat response regardless of specimen size or observed behavior patterns._

The incident shifted research dynamics considerably. Scientists who'd approached the valley variants with academic curiosity discovered that their subjects were actively hostile rather than passively dangerous. The controlled experiments continued, but with heightened caution and resigned acceptance that "controlled" was optimistic terminology when studying organisms evolved to consume everything in reach.

The valley provided regular opportunities for less controlled observation. Small antithesis incursions appeared with frustrating regularity—not the major hive incursions that triggered official Vanguard response, but the scattered, opportunistic infestations that made the valley's resistance characteristics simultaneously fascinating and dangerous.

Forest had developed a routine for dealing with minor incursions: locate the nascent hive, assess its development stage, and apply appropriate overwhelming force. The SPNKr remained his favorite tool — nothing quite matched the visceral satisfaction of reducing alien horror to smoking crater with high-explosive munitions. But Autumn had been encouraging more varied approaches, her scientific curiosity warring with her protective instincts.

"The scientists want to observe eradication procedures," she informed him during one such cleanup operation. "Dr. Singh's team has deployed portable sensors that can capture the entire process from initial detection through terminal sanitization."

Forest considered while examining the latest hive location—a shallow cave in granite outcrop that would have provided excellent defensible shelter if the antithesis had been slightly more competent about biomass acquisition. As it was, they'd managed to consume some native undergrowth and several unlucky wombats, producing a collection of Model Threes and Fours that were impressively dangerous in theory but practically hobbled by insufficient nutrition.

"Fine. But they observe from safe distance, with protective equipment, and somebody competent stays with them in case things go wrong."

Dr. Morrison's team arrived with enough equipment to film a documentary, their portable sensors and recording devices suggesting they'd taken the documentation requirements very seriously. The materials scientist had transformed over the past weeks from bemused academic to genuinely engaged researcher, his earlier discomfort with manual labor replaced by enthusiasm for observing exotic materials under extreme conditions.

"We're particularly interested in the thermal characteristics of your phosphorous munitions," Morrison explained while setting up sensor arrays. "The temperature profiles during combustion should provide insight into effective countermeasures against antithesis biological processes."

"It makes them burn real good," Forest offered, shouldering the SPNKr with the casual competence of someone who'd reduced this procedure to routine. "Not sure what else you need to know."

But Morrison's questions revealed sophisticated understanding of what they were actually observing. The antithesis weren't just alien organisms—they were highly efficient biological machines that violated numerous thermodynamic assumptions derived from terrestrial biochemistry. Watching them consumed by weapons specifically designed to exploit their vulnerabilities provided rare opportunity to observe the failure modes of truly alien biology.

The eradication itself was anticlimactic from a dramatic perspective — three high-explosive rockets delivered with methodical precision, reducing the hive from active threat to sanitized crater in approximately ninety seconds. But Morrison's sensors captured extraordinary detail: the thermal bloom as phosphorous ignited alien tissue, the chemical breakdown of exotic proteins under extreme heat, the collapse of biological structures that shouldn't have worked according to terrestrial chemistry but somehow did until they very abruptly didn't.

"Fascinating," Morrison breathed, reviewing sensor data with the enthusiasm of someone watching rare phenomena. "The thermal propagation suggests their cellular structures use coordinated quantum effects for metabolic processes. The phosphorous disrupts the quantum coherence, triggering cascade failure—"

"It makes them burn," Forest repeated, but he was smiling. Morrison's academic excitement was infectious, his genuine curiosity about mechanisms making the science interesting despite Forest's preference for practical results over theoretical understanding.

The pattern repeated over following weeks. Minor incursions appeared with frustrating regularity, each one providing opportunities for scientists to observe, document, and gradually build comprehensive understanding of how antithesis biology actually worked under field conditions. The textbook knowledge derived from laboratory studies proved inadequate for predicting behavior in the valley's harsh environment, where the alien organisms struggled against nutrient scarcity and actively hostile native ecology.

Dr. Kellerman's xenobiology research began revealing patterns that fascinated Autumn and horrified the scientists. The valley variants weren't just adapted for low-nutrient environments—they were evolving considerably faster than standard antithesis, selective pressure accelerating mutation rates in desperate search for viable strategies.

"They're learning," Kellerman explained during one of the communal dinners that had become valley routine. "Or rather, evolving behaviors that approximate learning. The hives are testing different approaches—some focus on rapid growth, others on efficient resource extraction, some on producing more dangerous combat variants despite limited biomass."

"And they're all failing," Janna observed, her nurse practitioner training making her comfortable with biological systems. "The eucalypts are too toxic, the soil's too poor, and we keep killing them before they can establish."

"Exactly. But the interesting question is: what happens when one of them finally succeeds?" Kellerman's concern was evident. "Natural selection is incredibly efficient given enough iterations. Eventually something will find a viable strategy."

The valley's residents absorbed this information with the pragmatic concern of people who understood that threats required response rather than panic. The Macintyre boys increased their patrols, treating antithesis cleanup like pest control—necessary maintenance rather than existential crisis. Oleksiy reinforced his property's perimeter with the methodical thoroughness of someone who'd survived by preparing for contingencies. Trix quietly ensured everyone had access to basic protective equipment and emergency medical supplies.

Forest found himself training valley residents in basic antithesis response. Not to transform them into combat specialists—that required Vanguard capabilities—but to provide survival skills for the increasingly common encounters. How to recognize hive characteristics, what behaviors indicated imminent threat, when to retreat versus when to defend, proper use of basic countermeasures.

The training sessions became community events, combining practical survival skills with social gathering. Trix provided food, Autumn offered tactical instruction through her presence drone, and gradually the valley's residents developed competence that went beyond academic understanding.

Dr. Chen proved surprisingly adept at improvised weapons, his academic precision translating well to grenade throwing and basic marksmanship. Dr. Morrison approached weapons training with the same systematic thoroughness he brought to materials analysis, treating each weapon as fascinating engineering challenge rather than tool for violence. Even Dr. Singh, whose field experience had included dangerous environments, appreciated the structured instruction in threats that existed outside her previous expertise.

The corporate representatives appeared occasionally, their visits marked by expensive vehicles and questions about timelines and deliverables. Ms. Thompson from Austral Defense Solutions seemed particularly interested in the weapons training, her defense contractor background making her appreciate the valley's transformation into what was effectively a fortified research outpost.

"You're building capability," she observed to Forest during one visit, watching valley residents practice with Flesh Melters under Autumn's instruction. "Not just research infrastructure—actual defensive capacity."

"We're teaching people how not to die," Forest corrected. "Seems like basic responsibility when we're studying things that want to eat them."

Thompson's smile suggested she appreciated the distinction while recognizing its commercial implications. A valley full of people competent in antithesis response was considerably more valuable than isolated scientists requiring constant protection. The research could expand, taking on greater risks, because the entire community was developing resilience.

Sophia Hachia's second visit coincided with a minor incursion that provided unfortunate demonstration of operational effectiveness. The Project Liaison had arrived unannounced, her timing either unfortunate or deliberate—Forest suspected the latter. She wanted to observe actual operations rather than sanitized reports.

The hive was barely established, perhaps a fortnight old, nestled in a ravine that provided shelter and moisture. It had managed to consume enough biomass to produce a dozen Model Threes and a handful of Fours, making it genuinely dangerous but well within Forest's capacity to handle.

What made the operation interesting was the participation. Dr. Chen served as spotter, his practiced eye identifying approach vectors and weak points from sensor data. The Macintyre boys provided perimeter security, their shotgun solid slugs adequate for handling any specimens that attempted flanking maneuvers. The kick from those things was ridiculous, but they were hefty lads. Dr. Morrison observed from fortified position with full sensor suite, capturing data that would inform future countermeasure development.

Forest approached from upwind, using terrain for cover and patience for advantage. The SPNKr remained his primary tool, but Autumn convinced him to try more surgical approaches when circumstances permitted. Three precisely placed Flesh Melters disabled the hive's defensive specimens, the chemical weapons proving brutally effective at ranges that made explosives inconvenient. Then the SPNKr finished the job, its high-explosive payload collapsing the ravine's overhang and burying the nascent hive under several tons of rock and burning phosphorous.

From detection to sanitization: eleven minutes. Zero casualties, comprehensive sensor data, and tactical demonstration that the valley's residents were considerably more capable than typical civilians.

Sophia observed in silence, her professional mask revealing nothing while she processed implications. After the cleanup, she accepted tea from Trix and addressed the assembled group with careful formality.

"This operation exceeded expectations for civilian response capability."

"We live here," Janna pointed out. "Seems reasonable to be competent at not dying."

The bureaucrat's smile was brief but genuine. "Competence is relative. What I observed was coordinated tactical response that would satisfy military training standards. The question becomes: is this replicable?"

Forest understood the real question. Sophia wasn't asking about training programs or standardized protocols—she was asking whether the valley's culture of practical competence could be exported, turned into policy, scaled beyond this isolated community.

"You can teach skills," Forest said carefully. "Weapons training, tactical awareness, threat recognition. But what makes us effective isn't the training—it's that we actually care about this place and each other. You can't regulate that into existence."

Sophia nodded, accepting the answer while clearly planning to try anyway. Bureaucracies existed to scale solutions, to transform local success into standardized policy. That the valley's effectiveness derived from organic community integration rather than imposed structure wouldn't prevent her from attempting replication.

After the Project Liaison departed, the valley returned to its new routine—research, training, communal meals, the gradual integration of scientific investigation with practical survival. The scientists were no longer visitors observing from external perspective. They were residents defending their home, contributing to a community that had adopted them despite initial intentions to maintain professional distance.

Autumn observed the transformation with the focused attention of someone recognizing emergent complexity from simple rules. Scientists and locals, originally separated by education and culture, had evolved collaborative relationship through shared meals, mutual training, and common purpose. The valley had become something more than research site or rural community—it was prototype for post-incursion social organization, combining academic expertise with practical competence, scientific investigation with survival necessity.

> _This is unprecedented,_ Autumn observed one evening. _Standard Protector protocols assume separation between civilian population and defense operations. Cooperation with PMCs isn't uncommon, but what you've created here is an integrated model where civilians are active participants in security infrastructure._

"We're just doing what makes sense," Forest replied. "The antithesis don't care about protocols or proper command structures. They show up and try to eat people. Seems reasonable that people should know how to respond."

> _Reasonable, yes. But also revolutionary. Most cities maintain strict separation between civilian population and Vanguard operations. Your valley residents are becoming a hybrid category. They are not combat specialists, but are far from helpless._

Forest considered the implications while watching the research station's lights illuminate the evening. The scientists were probably reviewing the day's data, their analysis informed by direct observation of what they studied. Trix would be planning tomorrow's meals, her hospitality weaving social bonds that made the community resilient. Oleksiy was likely reinforcing something, his builder's instincts ensuring structures would outlast the challenges they faced.

The valley wasn't his hidey-hole anymore. Transformed from refuge into something harder — laboratory and fortress, research site and killing ground — it had become a place where comfortable illusions went to die. They studied alien threats while learning to kill them efficiently. Academic investigation where failure meant being eaten alive.

Somewhere in the darkening bush, a hive was probably germinating, alien organisms testing new strategies in their desperate evolutionary race to find viable approach. And tomorrow, the valley's residents would locate it, document it, analyze it, and methodically reduce it to sanitized crater while gathering data that would inform humanity's larger struggle.

It was unprecedented, revolutionary, and — to Forest's mild surprise — _not_ the worst that could have happened.
